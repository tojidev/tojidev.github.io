A beginner's guide to Typography for the web

1 Set your paragraph text first

Content is king. Choose a font-family for the paragraphs <p> that honours the content. This will set the tone of your layout and create the base to set your headings and subheaders. To create contrast, combine a serif text with sans serif headings, or vice versa (more on combining fonts later). Try to limit your fonts to two styles maximum. Loading too many fonts will slow down your site.

¶ choose a comfortable type size for your paragraphs

The font-size of your paragraphs will determine how legible the text is. Digital screens need larger type sizes than text on a printed page. Also, a desktop screen requires a bigger typeface than a handheld device such as a tablet or a mobile phone.

The default paragraph text size in most desktop browsers is 16px, like this line.

Going below the default size of 16px is not advisable, as it might be too small for comfortable reading. Sizes ranging from 16px to 21px is standard.

¶ choose a proportional line-height

Running text viewed on the screen needs a bit more space between the lines than on a printed page. A line-height between 140% and 160% makes for comfortable reading on the screen. Wider paragraph widths will need some extra spacing between the lines, as do text set in a sans-serif type. The paragraphs in this page is set in 18px with a line-height of 150% or 1.5.

¶ do not make your paragraphs too wide

Paragraphs that are too wide make it difficult for the eye to find the start of the next line. The width of a paragraph should ideally be around 30 to 40 times the size of the type. The text on this page is set at 700px (~ 18 × 39) wide.

It is better to specify line-height as a percent (%) or a multiplyer (e.g. 140% = 1.4) of the font-size rather than in pixels. It will adjust itself proportionally when the font-size is changed.

2 Try a typographic scale for rythm

A typographic scale is a set of numbers related to each other in a certain ratio. It serves as a guide for choosing type sizes to create harmony. One such scale is the Fibonacci series, where each number is the sum of the previous two numbers (the numbers below denote pixels):

Choose a base font-size for your paragraphs from the series, e.g. 21px, and the larger numbers can be used for headings and subheads and the smaller numbers for secondary text and captions.

The type sizes in this page follow the Fibonacci series 4, 14, 18, 32, 50, 82 … You can also create your own scale or choose two different scales to add visual drama to your design.

Remember, a typographic scale is only a guide, not a necessity. You may choose not to use any at all, and depend completely on your own visual judgement.

Look up various typographic scales at modularscale.com

3 Large type may require some negative letterspace

E.g.

This is the default letterspace.

This line has a letterspace of −1px.

The default letter-spacing is often too loose for text set in large type. A little bit of negative letterspace is needed to make them right.

¶ add letterspace to uppercase letters

Letterspace small caps and uppercase letters. It increases readability and adds visual appeal and contrast to the page. Lowercase and italic do not require any letterspace in most cases.

Letterespace can be positive or negative. It is specified in pixels; e.g. 3px for positive and −3px for negative.

4 Use headings to mark the start of new content

Do not use headings <h1>, <h2>, etc indiscriminately. Use them to structure the content on a page. Be mindful of the heading hierarchy when designing a website; it's good practice not use the <h1> tag more than once in a page.

A heading or subhead should be distinct and stand out from the surrounding copy. A bold version of the paragraph font with ample space above and below should do the job. The top level headings can be in a larger size set to a typographic scale. Subheads set in bold or small caps can be the same size as the paragraph with space above and below to make them visually distinct.

The headings in this page are set at 32px. The subheads are set in small caps in the same size as the copy.

5 Combining fonts

Mixing fonts can be tricky business. Too many different fonts in a page create disharmony and chaos. It's best to stick to not more than two fonts; sticking to one is best and gets the job done in most cases. If combining fonts is necessary, try to select fonts that have similar x-heights. A serif and sans-serif can create contrast; but a bold and regular version of the same font can also achieve the same effect.

When mixing different fonts, it's a good practice to choose fonts designed by the same font designer. For instance Minion and Myriad (both designed by Robert Slimbach) or Perpetua and Gill Sans (by Eric Gill) complement each other, making them nice font pairs. Google Fonts provides useful suggestions on font pairings.

Mixing fonts from a 'superfamily' is also a good rule to follow. A 'superfamily' is a family of typefaces that consists of serif, sans, small caps etc. An example of a 'superfamily' is Alegreya, designed by Juan Pablo del Peral.

Beautiful Web Type has some very nice examples of font pairings from Google Fonts.

The paragraphs in this page have been set in Alegreya, the headings in Alegreya Sans, and the small caps in Alegreya SC, available from Google Fonts.
